# AI Daily Writing Methodology

This file is a public-source writing brief for Lumi. It is method guidance, not copy to quote.

## Current Lumi Style Lock

- Every text surface shares the same editorial backbone: concrete daily change -> plain-language problem -> technical mechanism -> product, cost, workflow, control, or reliability consequence.
- WeChat can carry the full descent; video narration should sound like the same editor speaking; video screen cards compress the same judgment into one visual bite; Bilibili should keep the same thesis without copying the article sentence by sentence.
- A hard technical term should rarely be the first explanation. First show the reader the work scene or bottleneck, then name the term, then translate the consequence.
- The title and cover should come from the same entity anchor as the article, but the cover must use a short visual version that can fully display.
- Openings and closings should be paired: the opening introduces one concrete detail or problem, the closing returns to it with a sharper judgment.
- The newest video style avoids meta-news openings such as “today's group of AI news” or “what I remembered first.” Start inside the user, product, or engineering scene instead.
- Video page titles and top progress labels must carry a visible subject. A viewer should know which company, model, paper, project, or platform the page is about before reading the rest; short progress labels can use recognizable abbreviations plus a hook.
- Screen cards should not be three equal summaries. Use one lead card for the core change and two smaller cards for mechanism and consequence, so the page has visual hierarchy and information density shifts.

## Editorial Bar

- Lead with a concrete change, not with the topic. A good opening lets the reader see what changed today before hearing the conclusion.
- Make every section earn its place. One story can explain the product surface, another can explain infrastructure cost, another can explain adoption friction; they should not all make the same point.
- Pair evidence with judgment. A paragraph should contain at least one named entity, dated event, number, technical detail, product behavior, or source-grounded scene before it asks the reader to accept a conclusion.
- Prefer explanation over slogan. If a sentence could fit any AI news day, replace it with the mechanism, workflow, cost, or user behavior that makes today different.
- Vary the editorial move. Across six stories, rotate between scene, contrast, mechanism, cost, platform position, developer consequence, and research implication.
- Close by compressing the day, not by repeating the list. The ending should make the reader feel the six stories now point to one useful frame.
- Build an explanation bridge before technical terms. A reader should understand the practical problem before seeing the label, acronym, paper method, benchmark name, chip name, or architecture term.
- Depth means a guided descent: everyday problem -> named technical mechanism -> why the mechanism changes cost, control, workflow, capability, or risk. Do not jump from term to conclusion.

## Source-Informed Practices

- OpenAI prompt guidance emphasizes clear instructions, separated context, concrete output examples, and replacing vague negatives with positive alternatives.
- OpenAI's public AI fundamentals writing starts with a simple map, everyday examples, and then introduces model terminology. It explains LLMs after first giving readers the category and product surface.
- Anthropic prompt guidance emphasizes relevant and diverse examples, prompt structure, and matching prompt style to desired output style.
- Anthropic's agent writing distinguishes terms before analysis: it first separates workflows from agents, then explains cost, latency, predictability, and flexibility tradeoffs.
- Google Vertex AI prompt guidance emphasizes role, context, examples, structured prompts, breaking complex tasks apart, and checking for redundant or conflicting instructions.
- Google DeepMind's technical blog often uses a concrete everyday analogy before the formal term, such as explaining reinforcement learning representations through a commute route before naming successor features.
- Content Marketing Institute's 2026 research points to relevance, content quality, team capability, and audience understanding as the main drivers of effective content.
- Top Chinese AI media such as 机器之心 are valuable because they combine technical detail, academic context, industry relevance, and high information density: they usually state the research or product change, explain what problem it solves, then place it in the broader field.
- Chinese AI industry writing such as 量子位 is strong at moving from a reader-facing question to market structure, then using concrete dimensions such as model, application, funding, team, ecosystem, or deployment path to keep abstraction grounded.

## Practical Moves

- Opening move: start from a concrete scene or contradiction, then widen to the day line.
- Technical move: explain the mechanism with one familiar analogy only when it clarifies the mechanism; otherwise use direct language.
- Term bridge move: before first use of a hard term, write the problem it solves in plain language; then name the term; then explain what changes because of it.
- Ladder move: for dense topics, use a three-step descent: user/work scene, system bottleneck, technical mechanism. The term appears only at step two or three.
- Product move: name who experiences the change first: developer, product manager, founder, infra buyer, researcher, enterprise operator, or end user.
- Industry move: explain what gets redistributed: attention, compute budget, integration work, distribution control, model access, data advantage, or trust.
- Transition move: make the next story answer a question left by the previous one, rather than using generic transitions.
- Closing move: leave one judgment the reader can repeat in a meeting.
- Reader checkpoint: after a technical paragraph, add one sentence that translates the mechanism into a consequence for product teams, developers, enterprises, investors, or ordinary AI users.
- Acronym discipline: if a section contains an acronym or architecture term, the same section should also contain the plain-language problem, a concrete affected actor, and a consequence.
